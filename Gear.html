Camping Gear

Getting Started

A major part of Scouting is going on Outings. Our Troop tries to have at least one outing every month, and Scouts need to be prepared for vigorous outdoor activities in all weather conditions.

There is a lot of excellent (and expensive) outdoor gear out there, but it makes sense to start smaller and less expensive. A new Scout isn't likely to carry their sleeping bag more than a mile or so — plenty of time to upgrade as they grow and get more serious about the outdoors.

Summer camps will often have their own packing list — check with the Leader and Parent Guide for the camp. High Adventure trips (open to older Scouts) will have specific gear requirements.

Many items are provided by the Troop, such as tents and cooking gear. The big personal items a Scout will need are: backpack, sleeping bag, sleeping pad, sturdy shoes, and appropriate clothing.

Clothing

Think layers and no cotton. Cotton is terrible when wet, it dries slowly and pulls heat away from your body, which is dangerous in cold environments. Stick with synthetics and wool. Regular clothes often work fine, especially when first joining.

  • T-shirt and long sleeve shirt
  • Long pants (zip-offs work great)
  • Wool socks
  • Warmth layers (thicker in winter, thinner in summer)
  • Sweater or fleece insulating layer
  • Wind-proof layer
  • Stocking cap and gloves
  • Waterproof rain jacket (not a poncho)
  • Rain pants (highly recommended in the Pacific Northwest)
  • Hat for sun or rain
  • Bandanna (multi-use — can substitute as a towel)
  • Swimming suit
  • Sleeping clothes (pajamas or t-shirt and shorts)

Hiking Boots & Shoes

Adults and Scouts who have stopped growing may want to invest in a good pair of sturdy medium to lightweight hiking boots. Younger Scouts can generally get by with less expensive options.

You'll want something that handles mud and snow. Inexpensive lightweight hiking boots are usually fine; younger Scouts can use inexpensive snow boots in winter. Sneakers and running shoes are generally not a good solution in wet or snowy conditions.

Inexpensive sneakers are a good choice as a second pair for summer camp and light activities in good weather, and water shoes work well on water outings.

Sleeping Bag

The sleeping bag is probably the single most expensive item a Scout needs. If a Scout gets serious about camping, they'll eventually want two — a lighter one for backpacking and summer, and a heavier one for snow camping. For now, a "good enough" bag works well.

The best compromise is a down bag rated to about 20°F. It'll be a little warm in summer (just unzip it) and marginal for winter (add extra clothes or a sleeping bag liner). It'll be a little heavy for backpacking, but will work in almost all conditions for a couple of years. Keep an eye out for sales and hand-me-downs.

Sleeping Pad

A sleeping pad is essential — it prevents heat loss from cold ground and is a key part of a Scout's sleeping system along with the bag and sleeping clothes.

Most Scouts go with a self-inflating pad. The Therm-a-Rest Trail Scout is a solid, affordable choice that can last an entire Scouting career. High-density foam pads like the Therm-a-Rest Z Lite are also effective and very reasonably priced.

Two major brands to look at: Therm-a-Rest and Klymit. Klymit occasionally does road shows at local Costcos with prices that beat their website.

Backpack

A Scout just needs a pack big enough and comfortable enough to carry gear for a couple miles. You'll probably end up in the 50–60 liter range. No need for an expedition pack — there are plenty of good options in the $100–200 range. Keep an eye out for sales.

A good way to size a pack: make a pile of all the personal gear you're going to take (sleeping bag, pad, clothes, etc.), stuff it into a sack to measure the volume, then increase that by 50–100% to account for food, water, and shared Troop gear.

Note: REI rents gear, which is a great way to try something before buying. REI often allows the rental cost to be applied toward a purchase — confirm details with REI first.

Other Gear

  • Daypack — a school backpack works, but may be bigger than needed
  • Ten Essentials — check the Scout Handbook or this Scouting Magazine article
  • Bowl, cup, and spoon — necessary; plate and fork are optional; a pocket knife can serve as an eating knife

Wait to Purchase

Some equipment is supplied by the Troop and doesn't need to be purchased right away. Eventually, if a Scout really enjoys the outdoors, they may want their own — but wait until you know more before buying:

  • Tent
  • Cooking gear (stove, pots, pans, etc.)
  • Water filter
